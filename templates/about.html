About Page

In the vivid tapestry of fantasy literature, Thorin and his company stand out as a memorable ensemble from J.R.R. Tolkien's world. Thorin Oakenshield, the stoic and courageous leader, heads this diverse group of dwarves on a perilous quest to reclaim their ancestral home from the clutches of the dragon Smaug. Each member of the company brings unique skills and traits to their journey, from the wise and observant Balin to the young and eager Fíli and Kíli. Together, they navigate the treacherous landscapes of Middle-earth, forging alliances and confronting enemies, driven by a shared dream of restoring their lost kingdom under the Lonely Mountain. Their story is a compelling blend of adventure, loyalty, and the pursuit of a homeland, making them enduring figures in the realm of epic fantasy.

Thorin Oakenshield

Thorin Oakenshield, a central figure in J.R.R. Tolkien's "The Hobbit," is a dwarf prince and a pivotal leader, embarking on a quest to reclaim the Lonely Mountain and its treasure from the dragon Smaug. Born into the royal line of Durin, Thorin is the grandson of King Thrór and the son of Thráin II, making him the rightful heir to the throne of Erebor. His epithet "Oakenshield" is derived from an incident during the Battle of Azanulbizar, where he used a branch of oak as a shield after his original was broken. Thorin is depicted as a complex character, driven by a strong sense of duty, honor, and a deep longing to restore his kingdom to its former glory. His leadership qualities are evident in his ability to rally his fellow dwarves and inspire loyalty, though his noble pursuit is often clouded by a growing obsession with the reclaimed treasure, which leads to moments of stubbornness and pride. Throughout "The Hobbit," Thorin's character develops significantly. Initially, he is wary and dismissive of Bilbo Baggins, the hobbit chosen by the wizard Gandalf to join their quest. However, as their journey progresses, Thorin grows to respect Bilbo's courage and ingenuity, recognizing his essential role in the success of their mission. Thorin's tragic flaw is his susceptibility to the "dragon-sickness" — a greed that overtakes him as he reclaims the treasure of Erebor. This greed strains his relationships with his allies and nearly leads to war against the Men of Lake-town and the Elves of Mirkwood. It is only towards the end of his life, after a series of dramatic and humbling events, that Thorin comes to realize the true value of friendship and honor over wealth. His final moments are marked by reconciliation and regret, as he seeks forgiveness from those he wronged. Thorin's legacy is one of a noble leader who, despite his faults, plays a crucial role in the resurgence of the Dwarven presence in Middle-earth, setting the stage for the events that unfold in "The Lord of the Rings." His story serves as a poignant reminder of the dangers of letting pride and material desires override more noble pursuits.

Fíli and Kíli

Fíli and Kíli are two of the youngest and most vibrant characters in J.R.R. Tolkien's "The Hobbit." They are brothers, nephews of Thorin Oakenshield, and key members of the company of dwarves that set out to reclaim the Lonely Mountain from the dragon Smaug. Born in the Blue Mountains, Fíli and Kíli are the sons of Dís, Thorin's sister, making them direct descendants of the royal line of Durin. **Fíli**, the elder of the two, is often described as having long, golden hair and a blue cloak. He is considered one of the best fighters in the group. Fíli is responsible and cautious, often taking a leadership role, especially in guiding and protecting his younger brother. His skills with weapons, particularly in archery and sword fighting, are noted throughout their adventures. **Kíli**, younger than Fíli by five years, shares his brother's adventurous spirit and bravery. He is described as having sharp eyesight, which makes him a good archer. Kíli's personality is more playful and light-hearted compared to Fíli's more serious demeanor. This difference adds a layer of depth to their relationship, showcasing a balance of youthfulness and responsibility. Both brothers play significant roles in the journey to the Lonely Mountain, where their skills, bravery, and brotherly bond are put to the test. They are integral in scouting missions and in several key battles against orcs and other foes. Their close relationship with each other and their uncle Thorin highlights themes of family loyalty and courage. Tragically, both Fíli and Kíli meet their end during the Battle of Five Armies, defending their wounded uncle Thorin from enemy forces. Their deaths are poignant in the narrative, emphasizing the harsh realities of their quest and the sacrifices made for their cause. They die as heroes, exemplifying the courage and spirit of the dwarf race. Their demise deeply affects Thorin, who regrets the conflict over gold that led to such losses, and they are mourned as symbols of the younger generation lost to the ravages of greed and war.
